Koryo Saram

Origin

The term "Koryo Saram" (고려 사람) refers to a community of ethnic Koreans who were historically deported or migrated to various regions in Central Asia, particularly Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Kyrgyzstan. The name "Koryo Saram" translates to "Korean people" or "people of Koryo" in Korean. The Koryo Saram community traces its roots back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries when many Koreans migrated to the Russian Far East seeking economic opportunities. However, during the Soviet era, particularly under Stalin's rule, there were instances of forced deportation of certain ethnic groups, including Koreans, to Central Asia.

Deportation

In the early 20th century, prior to the separation of Korea, numerous North Korean farmers resided near the border of Russia and Korea; particularly in Vladivostok. In 1937 during the conflict between Russia and Japan, suspicions arose among Russian authorities regarding Japanese espionage efforts through Northern Korean spies. As a result, Stalin initiated a mass deportation of "targeted nationalities," alleging espionage and disloyalty. The harrowing tales of this period have been passed down through generations, including the fateful day when the Russian military ordered all residents to assemble at the town hall; those who dared to question or speak out were executed on the spot or were taken away. Subsequently, 171,781 Koreans were forcibly placed onto cattle cars set for Central Asia, enduring a cruel 30 to 40-day journey marked by extreme hardships, resulting in the loss of over a third of their population.
Following Stalin’s death, in 1953, the government initiated a process of political rehabilitation for individuals who had been unjustly repressed and prosecuted under false pretenses. As part of this initiative, a document was disseminated to all Koryo-Saram families, affirming their status as an oppressed group.

Settlement

Upon arrival in their new land, they faced a multitude of challenges. Despite enduring harsh conditions, many survived with the assistance of Uzbek or Kazakh locals who offered them food and shelter. They managed to establish villages, known as Kalhoze. Within these communities, a cultural fusion emerged, blending Korean traditions with Uzbek or Kazakh influences and Russian customs. Unfortunately, with each generation, these traditions change or disappear. It is now the responsibility of the newer generations to uphold our cultural heritage and ensure its continuity through generations
